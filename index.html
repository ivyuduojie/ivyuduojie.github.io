Reducing Default Risk in a Digital Lending Product

Product Manager — United Bank for Africa · Lagos, Nigeria

The Problem

UBA Click Credit provided fast, unsecured consumer loans through mobile channels. Default rates consistently exceeded the bank's internal portfolio risk threshold, prompting the credit team to periodically recommend suspending the product.

Lending Workflow — Gap Identified

Loan Application
Eligibility Check
Employer Verification ← gap: not reliably captured
Credit Decision ← manual, inconsistent
Disbursement

Internal

Risk threshold breached

3

Root causes identified

Maintained

Product availability

Root Causes

Employer details not reliably captured at onboarding — limiting borrower traceability after disbursement

Uncollateralised loans relied on employer data as implicit collateral — not enforced by the product workflow

Manual credit decisioning created backlogs and inconsistent approval standards

My Contribution

Identified the employment verification gap as the primary default driver

Mapped the full lending workflow with credit risk, compliance, and operations teams

Proposed employer capture, salary-based eligibility validation, and borrower risk segmentation

Reframed internal discussion from product suspension to risk control improvement

Impact

Product remained operational while controls were strengthened

Improved borrower traceability through employer capture proposal

Reduced risk exposure through segmentation framework

Cross-functional alignment achieved between product and credit risk teams

Key Insight

The real problem was not borrower behaviour — it was missing identity signals inside the lending product architecture. Fixing data capture at onboarding was the highest-leverage intervention available.

Risk analysis · Stakeholder collaboration · Credit workflow mapping · Product requirements

Enterprise · Process Design · Unilever UK

Digital Canteen Ordering System

Business Analyst — 10Alytics (Remote) · Team: POD Lyra

The Problem

At Unilever's UK offices, 1,500 employees shared 300 canteen seats during a single lunch hour — spending 30–35 minutes queuing and as few as 10 minutes eating. The kitchen prepared food based on estimates, causing shortages and waste.

Process — Before & After

Before

→Employee arrives at canteen
→Joins queue (30–35 min)
→Orders at counter
→Waits for preparation
→10 min to eat

After

→Pre-order before 11am
→Kitchen prepares to demand
→Meal delivered to desk
→Full break to eat
→Payroll auto-deducted

30–35 min

Daily queue time saved

2,600%+

Projected Year 1 ROI

< 1 month

Payback period

My Contribution

Stakeholder analysis across 12 operational roles with influence/interest matrix

User stories defined for employee, kitchen, payroll, and management roles

Functional requirements documented and sprint backlog managed in Jira across 5 sprints

Business case built quantifying £4.8M–£5.6M projected annual productivity value

Impact

750–875 employee-hours recovered daily

20–30% projected reduction in food waste

Full system designed and approved for phased rollout

ROI payback period under one month

Key Insight

The core problem was not food production capacity — it was the absence of demand visibility. Pre-ordering shifted the system from reactive to planned, unlocking disproportionate value from a simple workflow change.
